%% ====================================================================
\section{Problem Setup}
%% ====================================================================

We have two discounted-cost MDPs sharing the same state space
$\mathcal{S} = \{-\Smax, \ldots, \Smax\}$ and action space
$\mathcal{A} = \{0, \ldots, \Smax\}$:
%
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Parameter & True model $M$ & Approximate model $\hat M$ \\
\midrule
Discount factor $\gamma$ & $0.75$ & $0.75$ \\
Demand $D \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n,q)$ & $n=10 ; q=0.4$ & $n=10 ; q=0.5$ \\
Holding cost $c_h$ & $4$ & $3.8$ \\
Shortage cost $c_s$ & $2$ & $2$ \\
Ordering cost $p$ & $5$ & $5$ \\
$\Smax$ & $500$ & $500$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The per-step cost in each model is
\[
  c(s,a) = p \cdot a + h(s),
  \qquad
  h(s) = \begin{cases} c_h \, s & s \ge 0, \\ -c_s \, s & s < 0. \end{cases}
\]
The transition kernel is deterministic given the demand realisation~$D$:
\[
  s' = \clip(s + a - D ; -\Smax ; \Smax).
\]

We denote by $\pistar$ and $\pihatstar$ the optimal policies of $M$ and $\hat M$
(both are base-stock policies with levels $\sigma^\star = 2$ and
$\hat\sigma^\star = 3$), and by $\Vstar$, $\Vhatstar$, $\Vpihat$ the
corresponding value functions.  Our goal is a \emph{state-dependent} bound
$\alpha \colon \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ satisfying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:goal}
  2\ alpha(s) \;\ge\; \Vpihat(s) - \Vstar(s) \qquad \forall\, s \in \mathcal{S}.
\end{equation}

%% ====================================================================
\section{Inputs Required}
%% ====================================================================

Before the sample-path bound computation we need the following, all obtained
via value iteration on the two models:
%
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $\Vhatstar(s)$ for all $s$ \quad (optimal value function of $\hat M$),
  \item $\pistar(s)$ for all $s$ \quad (optimal policy of $M$) ---
        \textbf{only needed for $\alpha_{\max}$}; the $\alphares$ and
        $\alpha_{\sup}$ variants do not require it,
  \item $\pihatstar(s)$ for all $s$ \quad (optimal policy of $\hat M$),
  \item $W_M[w] = \Pr_M(D = w)$ and $W_{\hat M}[w] = \Pr_{\hat M}(D = w)$
        for $w = 0, \ldots, n$ \quad (demand PMFs),
  \item $\Sigma$: a set of candidate base-stock levels containing
        $\sigma^\star$ --- \textbf{only needed for $\alphares$};
        defaults to $\{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$.
\end{enumerate}

%% ====================================================================
\section{Step 1: Cost Mismatch $\varepsilon(s)$}
%% ====================================================================

The cost mismatch at state $s$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:epsilon}
  \varepsilon(s)
  \;=\; \bigl|c_M(s, a) - c_{\hat M}(s, a)\bigr|
  \;=\; \bigl|h_M(s) - h_{\hat M}(s)\bigr|
\end{equation}
where the second equality uses the fact that the ordering cost $p$ is identical
in both models, so $p\,a$ cancels.  Note that $\varepsilon$ depends only on
$s$, not on the action~$a$.

Substituting the holding/shortage costs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:epsilon-explicit}
  \varepsilon(s) =
  \begin{cases}
    |c_h - \hat c_h| \cdot s = |4 - 3.8| \cdot s = 0.2\,s
      & \text{if } s \ge 0, \\[4pt]
    |c_s - \hat c_s| \cdot |s| = |2 - 2| \cdot |s| = 0
      & \text{if } s < 0.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 94--97:
\begin{verbatim}
    h_M  = M.h_vec(states)       # h_M(s) for all s
    h_Mh = M_hat.h_vec(states)   # h_Mhat(s) for all s
    epsilon = np.abs(h_M - h_Mh) # shape (num_states,)
\end{verbatim}
Output: a vector $\varepsilon \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}|}$ indexed by
state.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Step 2: Transition Mismatch $\Delta(s,a)$}
%% ====================================================================

The transition mismatch measures how differently the two models propagate
$\Vhatstar$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta}
  \Delta(s,a)
  \;=\; \Bigl|\sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} \Vhatstar(s')\, P_M(s' \mid s, a)
        \;-\; \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} \Vhatstar(s')\, P_{\hat M}(s' \mid s, a)\Bigr|.
\end{equation}

%% ====================================================================
\section{Step 3: Fixed-Point Iteration for $\alpha$}
%% ====================================================================

We now compute $\alpha(s)$ via fixed-point iteration.  We first present the
direct ``Q-function style'' formulation that matches the paper's notation
exactly, then show how the H-array trick makes it computationally tractable.

\subsection{Direct formulation (Q-function version)}
\label{sec:q-version}

This version uses the full transition kernel $P(s' \mid s, a)$ and follows
the MDP corollary of Theorem~2 directly.

\paragraph{Precomputation (done once).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cost mismatch} (a vector over states):
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:eps-q}
    \varepsilon(s) = |c_M(s,a) - c_{\hat M}(s,a)| = |h_M(s) - h_{\hat M}(s)|
    \qquad \forall\, s \in \mathcal{S}.
  \end{equation}

  \item \textbf{Transition mismatch} (a matrix over state--action pairs):
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:delta-q}
    \Delta(s, a) = \Bigl|
      \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} \Vhatstar(s')\, P_M(s' \mid s, a)
      - \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} \Vhatstar(s')\, P_{\hat M}(s' \mid s, a)
    \Bigr|
    \qquad \forall\, (s, a).
  \end{equation}
  In matrix notation, with $P$ and $\hat P$ denoting the $|\mathcal{S}| \times
  |\mathcal{A}| \times |\mathcal{S}|$ transition arrays:
  \[
    \boldsymbol{\Delta} = \bigl| P \hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star
    - \hat P \hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star \bigr|,
    \qquad \text{shape: } |\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|.
  \]
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Iteration (until convergence).}
Initialise $\alpha^0(s) = 0$ for all $s$.  At each iteration $k$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Propagate $\alpha^k$ through the true dynamics.}
For every state--action pair $(s, a)$, compute the expected future error
under the \textbf{true} model:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:propagate-q}
  \bigl[P_M \boldsymbol{\alpha}^k\bigr](s, a)
  \;=\; \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} \alpha^k(s')\, P_M(s' \mid s, a).
\end{equation}
This is a matrix--vector product: $P_M$ has shape $|\mathcal{S}| \times
|\mathcal{A}| \times |\mathcal{S}|$ and $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^k$ has shape
$|\mathcal{S}|$, giving a result of shape $|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|$.

\item \textbf{Compute the error certificate $\beta^k$.}
Assemble the three terms into a $|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|$ matrix:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-q}
  \boxed{%
    \beta^k(s, a)
    \;=\; \underbrace{\varepsilon(s)}_{\text{cost mismatch}}
    \;+\; \underbrace{\gamma \sum_{s'} \alpha^k(s')\, P_M(s' \mid s, a)}_{\text{propagated future error}}
    \;+\; \underbrace{\Delta(s, a)}_{\text{transition mismatch}}
  }
\end{equation}
In matrix notation:
\[
  \boldsymbol{\beta}^k
  = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \otimes \mathbf{1}_{|\mathcal{A}|}
  + \gamma\, P_M \boldsymbol{\alpha}^k
  + \boldsymbol{\Delta},
  \qquad \text{shape: } |\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|.
\]

\item \textbf{Update $\alpha$.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\alpha_{\max}$ (tighter): evaluate $\beta^k$ only at the two relevant
        actions:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-max-q}
    \alpha^{k+1}_{\max}(s) = \max\!\Big\{
      \beta^k\bigl(s , \pistar(s)\bigr) ;\;
      \beta^k\bigl(s , \pihatstar(s)\bigr)
    \Big\}.
  \end{equation}
  In code: indexing into the two columns of $\boldsymbol{\beta}^k$ at
  $\pistar(s)$ and $\pihatstar(s)$, then taking the element-wise max.

  \item $\alpha_{\sup}$ (looser): maximise over \emph{all} actions:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-sup-q}
    \alpha^{k+1}_{\sup}(s) = \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}}\, \beta^k(s, a).
  \end{equation}
  In code: $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{k+1}_{\sup} = \max_{\text{axis}=1}
  \boldsymbol{\beta}^k$.
\end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Check convergence}: stop if
$\max_s |\alpha^{k+1}(s) - \alpha^k(s)| < \texttt{tol}$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Pseudocode (Q-function version).}
\begin{verbatim}
    # Precompute (once)
    epsilon[s]   = |h_M(s) - h_Mhat(s)|         for all s       # |S| vector
    Delta[s, a]  = |P[s,a,:] @ V_hat_star        for all (s,a)   # |S| x |A|
                   - P_hat[s,a,:] @ V_hat_star|

    # Iterate
    alpha = zeros(|S|)
    for k = 0, 1, 2, ...:
        propagated[s,a] = P[s,a,:] @ alpha      for all (s,a)   # |S| x |A|
        beta[s,a] = epsilon[s] + gamma * propagated[s,a] + Delta[s,a]

        alpha_new[s] = max( beta[s, pi_star[s]], beta[s, pi_hat_star[s]] )
        if max|alpha_new - alpha| < tol: break
        alpha = alpha_new
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
The bottleneck is the propagation step~\eqref{eq:propagate-q}: multiplying
the $|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}| \times |\mathcal{S}|$ transition
array by the $|\mathcal{S}|$-vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^k$.  With
$|\mathcal{S}| = 1001$ and $|\mathcal{A}| = 501$:
\[
  \text{storage: } 1001 \times 501 \times 1001 \approx 5 \times 10^8
  \text{ entries} \approx 4\ text{GB (float64)},
\]
\[
  \text{per-iteration cost: } O(|\mathcal{S}|^2 \cdot |\mathcal{A}|)
  \approx 5 \times 10^8 \text{ multiplications}.
\]
This is why we use the H-array that is introduced in the next section.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Step 4: The H-Array Trick (Efficient Implementation)}
\label{sec:h-array}
%% ====================================================================

The Q-function version (Section~\ref{sec:q-version}) is conceptually clean
but computationally infeasible at $\Smax = 500$.  We now show how the
inventory MDP's structure reduces every transition-matrix operation to a
one-dimensional convolution, eliminating the need to store or multiply $P$.

\subsection{Key observation: post-order inventory}

In the inventory MDP, the next state is $s' = \clip(s + a - D)$ where
$D \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n, q)$ has support $\{0, \ldots, n\}$.  For any function
$f$ over states:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-array-identity}
  \sum_{s'} f(s')\, P(s' \mid s, a)
  = \sum_{w=0}^{n} W[w] \cdot f\bigl(\clip(s + a - w)\bigr).
\end{equation}
The right-hand side depends on $s$ and $a$ \textbf{only through the sum
$z = s + a$}, which is the \emph{post-order inventory level} (inventory after
ordering, before demand).  We precompute the \textbf{H-array}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-array}
  H_f[z] = \sum_{w=0}^{n} W[w] \cdot f\bigl(\clip(z - w)\bigr),
  \qquad z \in \{-\Smax , \ldots , 2\Smax\},
\end{equation}
so that $\sum_{s'} f(s') P(s' \mid s, a) = H_f[s+a]$.

\begin{remark}
The index $z$ ranges over $\{-\Smax, \ldots, 2\Smax\}$ because
$s + a \in \{-\Smax + 0, \ldots, \Smax + \Smax\}$.  The H-array has length
$3\Smax + 1 = 1501$, compared to the $1001 \times 501 = 501{,}501$ entries of
the full $[P \mathbf{f}](s,a)$ matrix.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Mapping each Q-function step to its H-array equivalent}

We now show how each step from Section~\ref{sec:q-version} translates.

\paragraph{Transition mismatch.}
The Q-function version computes $\Delta(s,a) = |[P_M \hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star](s,a) -
[P_{\hat M} \hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star](s,a)|$.  Using H-arrays:
\begin{align}
  H_M[z] &= \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_M[w] \cdot \Vhatstar\bigl(\clip(z-w)\bigr),
  \label{eq:HM} \\
  H_{\hat M}[z] &= \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_{\hat M}[w] \cdot \Vhatstar\bigl(\clip(z-w)\bigr),
  \label{eq:HMhat}
\end{align}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-delta}
  H_\Delta[z] = |H_M[z] - H_{\hat M}[z]|,
  \qquad
  \Delta(s,a) = H_\Delta[s+a].
\end{equation}
Instead of an $|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|$ matrix, we store a single
vector of length $3\Smax + 1$.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 100--107:
\begin{verbatim}
    H_M  = np.zeros(H_len)   # H_len = 3*s_max + 1
    H_Mh = np.zeros(H_len)
    for z in range(-s_max, 2*s_max + 1):
        for w in range(n_demand):
            ns = min(max(z - w, -s_max), s_max)
            H_M[z + s_max]  += W_M[w]  * V_hat_star[ns + s_max]
            H_Mh[z + s_max] += W_Mh[w] * V_hat_star[ns + s_max]
    H_delta = np.abs(H_M - H_Mh)
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Propagation of $\alpha$.}
The Q-function version computes $[P_M \boldsymbol{\alpha}^k](s,a) = \sum_{s'}
\alpha^k(s') P_M(s'|s,a)$.  Using the H-array:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h-alpha}
  H_\alpha[z] = \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_M[w] \cdot \alpha^k\bigl(\clip(z-w)\bigr),
  \qquad
  \sum_{s'} \alpha^k(s') P_M(s'|s,a) = H_\alpha[s+a].
\end{equation}
One vector of length $3\Smax + 1$ replaces the full $|\mathcal{S}| \times
|\mathcal{A}|$ matrix--vector product.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 115--119:
\begin{verbatim}
    H_alpha = np.zeros(H_len)
    for z in range(-s_max, 2*s_max + 1):
        for w in range(n_demand):
            ns = min(max(z - w, -s_max), s_max)
            H_alpha[z + s_max] += W_M[w] * alpha[ns + s_max]
\end{verbatim}

\begin{remark}[Why the true model?]
The propagation uses $W_M$ (not $W_{\hat M}$) because $\alpha(s)$ bounds the
suboptimality gap under the \emph{true} model's dynamics.  The approximate
model's influence is already captured in $H_\Delta$.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Error certificate $\beta$.}
The Q-function formula~\eqref{eq:beta-q} becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta}
  \boxed{%
  \beta(s, a)
  \;=\; \underbrace{\varepsilon(s)}_{\text{cost mismatch}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\gamma \cdot H_\alpha[s + a]}_{\text{propagated future error}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{H_\Delta[s + a]}_{\text{transition mismatch}}
  }
\end{equation}
This is identical to~\eqref{eq:beta-q}, but with $H_\alpha[s+a]$ replacing
$\sum_{s'} \alpha^k(s') P_M(s'|s,a)$ and $H_\Delta[s+a]$ replacing
$\Delta(s,a)$ --- both are just look-ups into precomputed arrays.

\paragraph{Update of $\alpha$.}

\subparagraph{Variant 1: $\alpha_{\max}$ (tighter).}
Maximise only over the two relevant policies:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-max}
  \alpha^{k+1}_{\max}(s)
  \;=\; \max\!\Big\{
    \beta\bigl(s , \pistar(s)\bigr) ;\;
    \beta\bigl(s , \pihatstar(s)\bigr)
  \Big\}.
\end{equation}
This suffices for the bound~\eqref{eq:goal} because the suboptimality gap
$\Vpihat(s) - \Vstar(s)$ involves only these two policies.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 122--131:
\begin{verbatim}
    alpha_new_max = np.zeros(num_states)
    for i, s in enumerate(states):
        s_int = int(s)
        best = -inf
        for a in [pi_star[i], pi_hat_star[i]]:
            z_idx = min(s_int + a, 2*s_max) + s_max
            beta_sa = epsilon[i] + gamma * H_alpha[z_idx] + H_delta[z_idx]
            if beta_sa > best:
                best = beta_sa
        alpha_new_max[i] = best
\end{verbatim}

\subparagraph{Variant 2: $\alpha_{\sup}$ (looser).}
Maximize over \emph{all} actions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-sup}
  \alpha^{k+1}_{\sup}(s)
  \;=\; \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}}\, \beta(s, a)
  \;=\; \varepsilon(s)
  \;+\; \max_{a = 0, \ldots, \Smax}
        \bigl[\gamma \cdot H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]\bigr].
\end{equation}
The second equality uses the fact that $\varepsilon(s)$ does not depend on~$a$.
For a given state $s$, the maximization ranges over $z = s + a$ for
$a = 0, \ldots, \Smax$, i.e., $z \in \{s, \ldots, s + \Smax\}$.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 137--144:
\begin{verbatim}
    combo = gamma * H_alpha + H_delta    # shape (H_len,)
    alpha_new_sup = np.zeros(num_states)
    for i, s in enumerate(states):
        s_int = int(s)
        z_lo = s_int + s_max             # array index for a=0
        z_hi = min(s_int + s_max, 2*s_max) + s_max  # for a=s_max
        alpha_new_sup[i] = epsilon[i] + np.max(combo[z_lo : z_hi + 1])
\end{verbatim}

\subparagraph{Variant 3: $\alphares$ (restricted action set).}

The $\alpha_{\max}$ variant requires the true optimal policy $\pistar$, while
$\alpha_{\sup}$ searches over all $|\mathcal{A}| = \Smax + 1$ actions.  The
\emph{restricted} variant provides a middle ground by exploiting
\textbf{structural knowledge about the policy class} without requiring
$\pistar$ itself.

For the inventory MDP, classical results from inventory theory guarantee that
the optimal policy is a \emph{base-stock policy}:
\[
  \pistar(s) = \max(0,\; \sigma^\star - s)
\]
for some base-stock level $\sigma^\star \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \Smax\}$.
Furthermore, the optimal base-stock level satisfies $\sigma^\star \le n$,
where $n = 10$ is the maximum possible demand (the support of the Binomial
distribution).  At any base-stock level $\sigma > n$, shortage is impossible
since $D \le n < \sigma$, so the only effect of increasing $\sigma$ further is
higher holding and ordering costs.  Combined with $\sigma^\star \ge 0$:
\[
  \sigma^\star \in \Sigma = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n\} = \{0, 1, \ldots, 10\}.
\]

The restricted action set at each state is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:restricted-action-set}
  A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)
  \;=\; \bigl\{\max(0, \sigma - s) : \sigma \in \Sigma\bigr\}
        \;\cup\; \bigl\{\pihatstar(s)\bigr\},
\end{equation}
which is guaranteed to contain $\pistar(s)$ (since $\sigma^\star \in \Sigma$)
and $\pihatstar(s)$ (since $\hat\sigma = 3 \in \Sigma$).  Hence
$\{\pistar(s), \pihatstar(s)\} \subseteq A_{\mathrm{res}}(s) \subseteq
\mathcal{A}$, and the Theorem~2 condition is satisfied.

The size of the restricted set is small: $|A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)| \le
|\Sigma| + 1 = 12$ for all $s$, compared to $|\mathcal{A}| = 501$ for
$\alpha_{\sup}$.

The update rule is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-restricted}
  \alpha^{k+1}_{\mathrm{res}}(s)
  \;=\; \max_{a \in A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)}\, \beta(s, a)
  \;=\; \varepsilon(s)
  \;+\; \max_{a \in A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)}
        \bigl[\gamma \cdot H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]\bigr].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{H-array optimisation for the restricted variant.}
Each candidate action $a = \max(0, \sigma - s)$ maps to post-order level
$z = s + a = s + \max(0, \sigma - s) = \max(s, \sigma)$.  The candidate
post-order levels therefore simplify by case:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{$s \ge n$}: all candidates satisfy $\sigma \le n \le s$, so
        $\max(s, \sigma) = s$ for every $\sigma \in \Sigma$.  The only
        candidate is $z = s$:
        \[
          \alphares^{k+1}(s) = \varepsilon(s) + \gamma H_\alpha[s] + H_\Delta[s].
        \]
  \item \textbf{$0 \le s < n$}: for $\sigma \le s$, $\max(s, \sigma) = s$;
        for $\sigma > s$, $\max(s, \sigma) = \sigma$.  The distinct candidate
        post-order levels are $\{s, s+1, \ldots, n\}$:
        \[
          \alphares^{k+1}(s) = \varepsilon(s) + \max_{z \in \{s, \ldots, n\}}
          \bigl[\gamma H_\alpha[z] + H_\Delta[z]\bigr].
        \]
  \item \textbf{$s < 0$}: $\max(0, \sigma - s) = \sigma - s > 0$ for all
        $\sigma \ge 0$, giving $z = \sigma$.  The candidate post-order levels
        are $\Sigma = \{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$:
        \[
          \alphares^{k+1}(s) = \varepsilon(s) + \max_{\sigma \in \Sigma}
          \bigl[\gamma H_\alpha[\sigma] + H_\Delta[\sigma]\bigr].
        \]
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, function \texttt{sample\_path\_bound\_restricted},
inner loop:
\begin{verbatim}
    combo = gamma * H_alpha + H_delta    # shape (H_len,)
    alpha_new = np.zeros(num_states)
    for i, s in enumerate(states):
        s_int = int(s)
        if s_int >= n:
            # All base-stock candidates give a=0, z=s
            z_idx = s_int + s_max
            alpha_new[i] = epsilon[i] + combo[z_idx]
        elif s_int >= 0:
            # Candidate z values: {s, s+1, ..., n}
            z_lo = s_int + s_max
            z_hi = n + s_max
            alpha_new[i] = epsilon[i] + np.max(combo[z_lo:z_hi + 1])
        else:
            # s < 0: candidate z values are Sigma
            alpha_new[i] = epsilon[i] + np.max(combo[Sigma_arr + s_max])
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Ordering of bounds.}

By construction, $\alpha_{\max}(s) \le \alphares(s) \le \alpha_{\sup}(s)$ for all $s$, since
$\alpha_{\max}$ maximises over a \emph{subset} of the actions in $A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)$,
which is itself a subset of $\mathcal{A}$.

\paragraph{Convergence check.}

After computing $\alpha^{k+1}$, check:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:convergence}
  \delta^k \;=\; \max_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \bigl|\alpha^{k+1}(s) - \alpha^k(s)\bigr|
  \;<\; \texttt{tol} = 10^{-10}.
\end{equation}
If satisfied, terminate.  Otherwise, set $\alpha^k \leftarrow \alpha^{k+1}$ and
return to the propagation step.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
\texttt{bounds.py}, lines 146--152:
\begin{verbatim}
    diff = np.max(np.abs(alpha_new_max - alpha))
    history.append(diff)
    alpha = alpha_new_max
    if diff < tol:
        break
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Correspondence between the two formulations}

The table below summarises how each quantity in the Q-function version maps to
its H-array equivalent.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Quantity} & \textbf{Q-function version} & \textbf{H-array version} \\
\midrule
Transition mismatch
  & $\Delta(s,a) = |[P_M \hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star - P_{\hat M}\hat{\mathbf{V}}^\star](s,a)|$
  & $H_\Delta[s+a]$ \\
Propagated error
  & $[P_M \boldsymbol{\alpha}^k](s,a)$
  & $H_\alpha[s+a]$ \\
Error certificate
  & $\beta^k(s,a) = \varepsilon(s) + \gamma [P_M\boldsymbol{\alpha}^k](s,a) + \Delta(s,a)$
  & $\varepsilon(s) + \gamma H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]$ \\
Storage
  & $|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{A}|$ matrices ($\sim\!501$K entries)
  & vectors of length $3\Smax+1$ (1501 entries) \\
Per-iteration cost
  & $O(|\mathcal{S}|^2 \cdot |\mathcal{A}|) \approx 5 \times 10^8$
  & $O(n \cdot (3\Smax + 1)) \approx 1.7 \times 10^4$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The H-array trick exploits the fact that in the inventory MDP, $P(s'|s,a)$
depends on $(s,a)$ only through the post-order level $z = s + a$.  This
collapses the two-dimensional $(s,a)$ index into a single index $z$, reducing
storage by a factor of $\sim\!334\times$ and computation by a factor of
$\sim\!29{,}000\times$ per iteration.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Convergence Guarantee}
%% ====================================================================

Define the operator $\mathcal{T}\colon \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}|} \to
\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}|}$ by
\[
  (\mathcal{T}\alpha)(s)
  \;=\; \max_{a \in A(s)}
        \Bigl[\varepsilon(s) + \gamma \sum_{s'} \alpha(s')\, P_M(s' \mid s, a)
        + H_\Delta[s+a]\Bigr],
\]
where $A(s) = \{\pistar(s), \pihatstar(s)\}$ for $\alpha_{\max}$,
$A(s) = A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)$ for $\alphares$~\eqref{eq:restricted-action-set},
and $A(s) = \mathcal{A}$ for $\alpha_{\sup}$.

For any $\alpha, \alpha' \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}|}$:
\begin{align*}
  \bigl|(\mathcal{T}\alpha)(s) - (\mathcal{T}\alpha')(s)\bigr|
  &\;\le\; \gamma \max_{a \in A(s)} \sum_{s'} \bigl|\alpha(s') - \alpha'(s')\bigr|
           \, P_M(s' \mid s, a) \\
  &\;\le\; \gamma \ |\alpha - \alpha'\|_\infty,
\end{align*}
since $P_M(\cdot \mid s, a)$ is a probability distribution.  Hence $\mathcal{T}$
is a $\gamma$-contraction in sup-norm, and by Banach's fixed-point theorem
\[
  \|\alpha^{k+1} - \alpha^\star\|_\infty
  \;\le\; \gamma^k \, \|\alpha^1 - \alpha^0\|_\infty.
\]
With $\gamma = 0.75$, reaching tolerance $10^{-10}$ requires roughly
$k \approx \log(10^{-10}) / \log(0.75) \approx 80$ iterations (in practice:
94 iterations).

%% ====================================================================
\section{Full Algorithm}
%% ====================================================================

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Sample-Path Dependent AIS Bound}
\label{alg:sample-path}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $M$, $\hat M$, $\Vhatstar$, $\pistar$, $\pihatstar$, $\Sigma$, tolerance $\texttt{tol}$
\Ensure $\alpha_{\max}$, $\alphares$, $\alpha_{\sup}$ such that
        $2\alpha(s) \ge \Vpihat(s) - \Vstar(s)$ for all $s$
\Statex
\State \textbf{Precompute cost mismatch:}
  $\varepsilon(s) \gets |h_M(s) - h_{\hat M}(s)|$ for all $s$
  \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:epsilon}}
\Statex
\State \textbf{Precompute transition mismatch H-array:}
\For{$z = -\Smax$ \textbf{to} $2\Smax$}
  \State $H_M[z] \gets \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_M[w] \cdot \Vhatstar(\clip(z-w))$
  \State $H_{\hat M}[z] \gets \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_{\hat M}[w] \cdot \Vhatstar(\clip(z-w))$
\EndFor
\State $H_\Delta[z] \gets |H_M[z] - H_{\hat M}[z]|$ for all $z$
  \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:h-delta}}
\Statex
\State \textbf{Fixed-point iteration:}
\State $\alpha^0(s) \gets 0$ for all $s$
\For{$k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$}
  \State Build $H_\alpha[z] \gets \sum_{w=0}^{n} W_M[w] \cdot \alpha^k(\clip(z-w))$
    for all $z$
    \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:h-alpha}}
  \For{each state $s$}
    \State $\alpha^{k+1}_{\max}(s) \gets
      \max_{a \in \{\pistar(s) , \pihatstar(s)\}}
      \bigl[\varepsilon(s) + \gamma H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]\bigr]$
      \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha-max}}
    \State $\alphares^{k+1}(s) \gets
      \max_{a \in A_{\mathrm{res}}(s)}
      \bigl[\varepsilon(s) + \gamma H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]\bigr]$
      \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha-restricted}}
    \State $\alpha^{k+1}_{\sup}(s) \gets
      \varepsilon(s) + \max_{a=0}^{\Smax}
      \bigl[\gamma H_\alpha[s+a] + H_\Delta[s+a]\bigr]$
      \hfill \textit{Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha-sup}}
  \EndFor
  \If{$\max_s |\alpha^{k+1}_{\max}(s) - \alpha^k(s)| < \texttt{tol}$}
    \State \textbf{break}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return $\alpha_{\max}$, $\alphares$, $\alpha_{\sup}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Let $N = 2\Smax + 1 = |\mathcal{S}|$ and $n = 10$ (demand support size).

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Step & Computed once & Per iteration \\
\midrule
Cost mismatch $\varepsilon$ & $O(N)$ & --- \\
Transition mismatch $H_\Delta$ & $O(n \cdot (3\Smax+1))$ & --- \\
Propagation H-array $H_\alpha$ & --- & $O(n \cdot (3\Smax+1))$ \\

With $\Smax = 500$, $n = 10$, and $\sim 94$ iterations, the total cost is
dominated by the $\alpha_{\sup}$ update at $O(N \cdot \Smax) \approx 5 \times

variant has per-iteration cost $O(N \cdot n) \approx 10^4$, comparable to the
H-array build step and roughly $50\times$ cheaper than $\alpha_{\sup}$.

The key saving is that no transition matrix (which would require
$O(N^2 \cdot |\mathcal{A}|) \approx 5 \times 10^8$ entries) is ever formed or
stored.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Error Decomposition at the Fixed Point}
%% ====================================================================

At convergence, $\alpha = \mathcal{T}\alpha$, so for the action
$a = \pihatstar(s)$ (which achieves the max at most states in the operating
region):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomposition}
  \alpha(s)
  \;=\; \underbrace{\varepsilon\bigl(s, \pihatstar(s)\bigr)}_{\text{cost mismatch}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\gamma \sum_{s'} \alpha(s')\, P_M\bigl(s' \mid s, \pihatstar(s)\bigr)}_{\text{propagated future error}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\Delta\bigl(s, \pihatstar(s)\bigr)}_{\text{transition mismatch on }\Vhatstar}.
\end{equation}

This decomposition reveals which source of model mismatch dominates at each
state:
\begin{itemize}
  \item For $s \ge 0$: the cost mismatch $\varepsilon(s) = 0.2\,s$ grows
        linearly, eventually dominating.
  \item For $s < 0$: the cost mismatch is zero ($c_s$ is identical), so the
        bound is driven entirely by the transition mismatch $\Delta$ and
        propagated error.
  \item Near the base-stock levels ($s \in \{-7, \ldots, 3\}$): the propagated
        future error $\gamma \mathbb{E}[\alpha(s')]$ is the dominant term, since
        the local cost and transition mismatches are small.
\end{itemize}

\section{Remark: When the True Transition Kernel Is Unknown}
%% ====================================================================

Both the sample-path bound and the weighted-norm bound from the model
approximation paper require access to the true model's one-step transition
probabilities~$P_M(s' \mid s, a)$.  Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The transition mismatch $\Delta(s,a)$ evaluates
        $\sum_{s'} \Vhatstar(s') P_M(s' \mid s, a)$.
  \item The propagation step computes
        $\sum_{s'} \alpha(s') P_M(s' \mid s, a)$.
  \item The weighted contraction coefficient $\kappa$ requires
        $\sum_{s'} w(s') P_M(s' \mid s, a)$.
\end{itemize}

In settings where $P_M$ is not available but one has structural knowledge
about how $P_M$ and $P_{\hat M}$ may differ, these quantities can be
\emph{upper bounded} without evaluating $P_M$ directly.  For the transition
mismatch, a standard bound via total variation distance gives:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tv-bound}
  \Delta(s,a)
  \;=\; \Bigl|\sum_{s'} \Vhatstar(s')\bigl[P_M(s'|s,a) - P_{\hat M}(s'|s,a)\bigr]\Bigr|
  \;\le\; \|\Vhatstar\|_\infty \cdot
          \bigl\|P_M(\cdot|s,a) - P_{\hat M}(\cdot|s,a)\bigr\|_{\mathrm{TV}},
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{TV}}$ denotes the total variation distance (half the
$L^1$ norm).  If one has an upper bound $\bar\delta(s,a)$ on the total
variation distance --- for example, from knowing that the model parameters lie
in some uncertainty set --- then~\eqref{eq:tv-bound} provides a computable
surrogate for $\Delta(s,a)$ that does not require evaluating $P_M$.

Similarly, the propagation term can be routed through $P_{\hat M}$ with a
correction:
\[
  \sum_{s'} \alpha(s') P_M(s'|s,a)
  \;\le\; \sum_{s'} \alpha(s') P_{\hat M}(s'|s,a)
  \;+\; \|\alpha\|_\infty \cdot
        \bigl\|P_M(\cdot|s,a) - P_{\hat M}(\cdot|s,a)\bigr\|_{\mathrm{TV}}.
\]

The resulting bounds are looser but remain valid, and they connect to the
robust/distributionally-robust MDP literature where one optimises over a
worst-case transition kernel within an uncertainty set around $P_{\hat M}$.
